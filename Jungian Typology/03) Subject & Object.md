# $$ \fbox{Chapter 2: Subject \& Object} $$





## I. Subject

When we are referring to a *subject*, we are actually referring to the entity who is powered through its *consciousness*. And consciousness is what processes contents from its observation. When we combine the subject and the consciousness, we get *ego*. Ego is what the individual identifies with, and calls it as *"I"* or *"me"*. A subject identifies itself with what its consciousness is aware of already.

To understand it this way, consider a group where there are five people gathered. One of which is *you*, and the names of the rest are John, Ming, Alex, and Rahul. So when writing it together the list becomes *you*, John, Ming, Alex, and Rahul. We can change the order in given arrangement, but that doesn't affect what our example for being an irrelevant factor in this study. *You* in your frame of reference, are the subject who is processing these writings by me. While if Ming reads this very same texts, he is the subject in his own frame of reference, from his conscious standpoint.



## II. Object

On the contrary to subject, an *object* is what's there other than the subject, the outer world or the contents which are being examined by the subject. The subject processes the object as its content. It is something the consciousness processes, but doesn't identify with primarily. Here we are not referring to identification of the ego, but about the consciousness. Ego can make claims which aren't true to preserve the stability of the subject. For example, identifying with a group as an object while subjects themselves having their own personal beliefs and values.

Taking the same example of the group and consider the same conditions as given in previous example, *you* the reader are the subject in your frame of reference. But who are the object here? The remaining people in the group who complete the whole system when included with subject, are the object. So John, Ming, Alex, and Rahul are the object, whether when taken individually or altogether. So Rahul alone is an object for you as the subject, who is consciously processing it, while the rest of the group taken together is also an object. If we see from Ming's frame of reference, then he is the subject, and rest of the people individually or in group, are objects.



## III. Subject & Object Involvement

This fact is worth noting that the subject alone, or an object alone is isolated and there is nothing to process at all. This is because the subject processes an object, which acts as a stimulus for them to process on. And similarly, an object alone has no significance if there is no subject to process it. Because how else do we know that there is an object? Who is watching and deciding if there is an object or not? Who is understanding what's the difference between the subject and the object? 

Consider subject as a machine whose work is to produce output, and object as the input. So subject with this analogy of machine processes object as an input, to produce an output. From the previous example, *you* as the subject were processing Ming, John, Rahul, and Alex (the inputs), as the subject (the machine). Without any input, what's the use  of machine? How will it process anything and expand its understanding or become applicable without any input? Similarly, what good is an input for when there is no machine no machine to process it? How do we know if there even exists any input to processed or not?

From same example, even if we remove *you* as the subject, there will be four people left there, which are Alex, Rahul, Ming, and John. Each of them can be considered as a subject from their own frame of reference and they can process rest of the three people as object, taken individually in parts, or together as a single unit. But talking from *your* frame of reference, there is not subject to experience or process anything, while the still object remain. Or they don't? There is no body to process that information.
