## I. Author Carl Jung's Intent


*Carl Gustav Jung*, the author of the book *Psychological Types*, worked closely with *Sigmund Freud* who is the father of field of *psychoanalysis*. Both worked together from 1907 to 1913. Freud viewed Jung as his successor. But both had later strong disagreement on the concept of psychic energy. Freud emphasized sexuality as the primary psychological force, while Jung proposed a broader concept of psychic energy and later developed ideas like archetypes and the collective unconscious. For this reason, both had to split separate ways.

After this split, Jung wondered why different people arrive at different conclusions. To understand this, he theorized that people had and valued different conscious orientations. He noticed the recurring patterns in his observation and tried to categorize them into certain primitives. Now we know those primitives as the *attitude types*, involved perceiving or judging *functions*, and the eight *psychological types* formed from combining them. Modern systems derived from Jung's original theory and its letter representation describe sixteen type, to emphasis on effect of *auxiliary function*. I will explain that very soon.

Point to note is that Jung originally never intended to create a formal system to categorize of type people. Instead they were proposed in order to understand differences and disagreements among individuals. This was also to help people grow beyond their orientation, and develop an understanding of different opinions. His intention was to categorize mode of processing or conscious orientation, not to categorize people into rigid boxes.



## II. Jungian Terminologies


- *Ego* is the identification of an entity with themselves, the sense of *"me"* or *"I"*.
- *Consciousness* is the energy that powers a subject for it to be able to perceive or judge what it encounters, the awareness of an entity or being.
- *Subject* is the conscious entity (you) who perceives or judges the content of consciousness.
- *Object* is what the subject perceives or judges. It can be anything, starting from another person or group to a larger system.
- *Attitude* is mode of orientation for a subject where it tends to either internally organize information (introversion), or adapt to requirements of present conditions (extroversion).
- *Function* is a form of processing primitive that tells us in terms of what a subject primarily values to process information.
- *Psychological types* are eight types of processing styles which are formed when we combine attitudes and functions.
- *Perceiving functions*, also known as *irrational functions*, are those functions which observe and register the extracted information from the object as it is.
- *Judging functions*, also known as *rational functions*, are those functions which evaluates the information that the object contains.



## III. Attitudes


#### *i. Introversion*

*Introversion* is one of the two attitudes of psyche to process information. Whoever has this form of primary attitude, their *libido* (conscious energy) flow inward towards the subject. The psyche of the subject irrespective of their natural orientation contains subjective contents, where the introverted individuals primarily focus on. This can be anything like past memories, learnings, personal principles, or subjective understanding, etc. They usually don't prefer to adapt to constantly changing conditions or trends in outside world, and rather choose to stick with what is suitable or matches to their subjective contents. To observe and understand an object, they would usually prefer to take a snapshot of the object, and internally organize or merge them with their existing contents. They might be rigid if something doesn't matches or contradicts with their subjective contents, as it is opposite to adapting attitude, but healthy introverts learn to integrate their *auxiliary function* (will be discussed later) to expand their understanding. It also doesn't mean that introverts just refer to operate on subjective contents, its just that its their primary orientation attitude. Secondary attitude of extroversion still exists, and accessible to them. And its definitely not about social introversion, but an orientation one. An introvert can have many friends and be social, but will prefer to stick with subjective factor instead of adapting.

For example, imagine a person who is **not** lazy, but gets a chance to travel New Zealand, but is too much used to be at hometown. So even if the object (trip to New Zealand) offers a thrilling and memorable experience to him, he refuses simply to remain in an environment which his psyche is used to and perfectly organized around subjectively. So he won't adapt to the object, and instead prefer to stick with the subjective factor, the environment he is familiar with. Now this is an extreme example, and certainly not the only form of introversion, but one type of it. There could be many types of introversion, like not changing personal opinion to adapt with external belief systems, ignoring obvious signs of failure just because someone never saw a system fail, and many more.
