## I. Author Carl Jung's Intent


*Carl Gustav Jung*, the author of the book *Psychological Types*, worked closely with *Sigmund Freud* who is the father of field of *psychoanalysis*. Both worked together from 1907 to 1913. Freud viewed Jung as his successor. But both had later strong disagreement on the concept of psychic energy. Freud emphasized sexuality as the primary psychological force, while Jung proposed a broader concept of psychic energy and later developed ideas like archetypes and the collective unconscious. For this reason, both had to split separate ways.

After this split, Jung wondered why different people arrive at different conclusions. To understand this, he theorized that people had and valued different conscious orientations. He noticed the recurring patterns in his observation and tried to categorize them into certain primitives. Now we know those primitives as the *attitude types*, involved perceiving or judging *functions*, and the eight *psychological types* formed from combining them. Modern systems derived from Jung's original theory and its letter representation describe sixteen type, to put emphasis on effect of *auxiliary function*. I will explain that very soon.

Point to note is that Jung originally never intended to create a formal system to categorize type of people. Instead they were proposed in order to understand differences and disagreements among individuals. This was also to help people grow beyond their orientation, and develop an understanding of different opinions. His intention was to categorize mode of processing or conscious orientation, not to categorize people into rigid boxes.



## II. Jungian Terminologies


- *Ego* is the identification of an entity with themselves, the sense of *"me"* or *"I"*.
- *Consciousness* is the energy that powers a subject for it to be able to perceive or judge what it encounters, the awareness of an entity or being.
- *Content of consciousness* is what the consciousness or awareness focuses or converges attention towards.
- *Subject* is the conscious entity (you) who perceives or judges the content of consciousness.
- *Object* is what the subject perceives or judges. It can be anything, starting from another person or group to a larger system.
- *Attitude* is mode of orientation for a subject where it tends to either internally organize information (introversion), or adapt to requirements of present conditions (extroversion).
- *Function* is a form of processing primitive that tells us in terms of what a subject primarily values to process information.
- *Psychological types* are eight types of processing styles which are formed when we combine attitudes and functions.
- *Perceiving functions*, also known as *irrational functions*, are those functions which observe and register the extracted information from the object as it is.
- *Judging functions*, also known as *rational functions*, are those functions which evaluates the information that the object contains.



## III. Attitudes


#### *i. Introversion*

*Introversion* is one of the two attitudes of psyche to process information. Whoever has this form of primary attitude, their *libido* (conscious energy) flows inwards to the subject. The psyche of the subject irrespective of their natural orientation contains subjective contents, where the introverted individuals primarily focus on. This can be anything like past memories, learnings, personal principles, or subjective understanding, etc. They usually don't prefer to adapt to constantly changing conditions or trends in outside world, and rather choose to stick with what is suitable or matches to their subjective contents. To observe or understand an object, they would usually prefer to take a snapshot of the object, and internally organize or merge them with their existing contents. They might be rigid if something doesn't matches or contradicts with their subjective contents, as it is opposite to adapting attitude, but healthy introverts learn to integrate their *auxiliary function* (will be discussed later) to expand their understanding. It also doesn't mean that introverts just refer to operate on subjective contents, its just that its their primary orientation attitude. Secondary attitude of extroversion still exists, and accessible to them. And its definitely not about social introversion. An introvert can have many friends and be social, but will prefer to stick with subjective factor instead of adapting.

For example, imagine a person who is **not** lazy, but gets a chance to shift to New Zealand for getting better business opportunity, however he is too much used to be at hometown. So even if the object (trip to New Zealand) offers a thrilling and memorable business experience to him, he refuses simply to remain in an environment which his psyche is used to and perfectly organized around subjectively. So he won't adapt to the object, and instead prefer to stick with the subjective factor, the environment he is familiar with. Now this is an extreme example, and certainly not the only form of introversion, but one type of it. There could be many types of introversion, like not changing personal opinion to adapt with external belief systems, ignoring obvious signs of failure just because someone never saw a system fail, and many more. An introvert grows when he or she learns to adapt with external world while not fully ignoring their subjective factor.


#### *ii. Extroversion*

*Extroversion* is the second type of attitude of psyche to process the information. With this type of primary attitude, the libido flows outwards to the object. While the psyche of an extroverted contains subjective contents or the subjective factors like an introvert, it relies on the objective factor more than the subjective factor. This objective factor is what's happening in outside world (outside their minds), and the present conditions. Their usual orientation is to adapt with the changing situations, conditions, and trends of the world or their surrounding environment, instead of sticking primarily to their subjective contents or factor. To understand the object, instead of taking snapshot and internally organizing like the introverted types, they read the situation in real-time and tend to quickly adapt with its requirements. Not that they never plan or always improvise, but they try to match with the objective factor. That requires them to respond to constantly changing external conditions. While introverts can have the ability to do the same, they usually won't prefer to because of their natural orientation. Orientation and preference are different terms, and *Classic Jungian Typology* is about orientation, not strength. Unlike introverts, they aren't rigid with their subjective factor and tend to comfortably change or adapt if that's what required of them in their present situation. But they can still be sometimes rigid because of their secondary introverted attitude coming up sometimes. Again, its not about social extroversion, but about orientation.

Taking the same example of business opportunity requiring a person to shift to New Zealand, an extrovert would be very adaptable and thus won't hesitate from shifting. Its not necessary that they are cold or detached from their hometown, but they value adaptability more than what they are subjectively used to. But we will further learn how an extrovert might not leave his hometown in same situation for entirely different reason, and why an introvert might leave it. For now, take these examples with a grain of salt, and not literally.



## IV. Functions


#### *i. Sensing*

*Sensing* is a one of the four functions, and one of the two *perceiving functions*. Its a way to process information while focusing on a particular aspect, i.e. sensation. Sensation is how something is perceived by the senses of a subject. Now most people limit it to five sensations (touch, sight, smell, taste, and sound) using five senses (skin, eyes, nose, tongue, and ears). But there is more to that, related sensations and those beyond them. Like how it feels internally of one's body when perceiving something. That sensation which brings euphoria, sorrow, adrenaline, or anything that evokes an internal sensation. And again its not exclusive to these sensations, but also the generally believed sensation through five senses. Now why is it a perceiving function and not judging one? Because sensation is about sensory perception of the contents in consciousness, not judging them or evaluating them on the basis of anything. Its simply registering what was observed, but extracting just the sensory information.

For example, imagine a person with primary orientation towards the sensing function is roaming around in a park. He notices a regular-sized rock near a shrub. He picks it up, and now he would process it by focusing on the sensory information that it provides. Like its look, color, shape, weight, etc. But **not** labelling how it looks, which color it has, which shape it has, etc, that would cross into the judging function territory. Whereas the perceiving functions are about simply registering what was seen or experienced, in object or within subject.


#### *ii. Intuition*

*Intuition* is the other perceiving function. And unlike sensation, it processes information based on what is implied beyond sensory details. Its not a mystical or magical function, it is something all of us use, but some have naturally dominant orientation towards this form of processing. It perceives the object or even subjective content, and perceives something that senses didn't perceived. Then who perceived it? Its the *unconscious*, a primal realization that perceives something you can't directly reach through sensory perception or through judging. It can sometimes resemble other functions and could be difficult to distinguish with, but its easier ones we understand why it is a perceiving function. So why is it a perceiving function? Because perception is about registering what it observed or experienced instead of being evaluated, and intuition comes as a sudden realization without steps or causal chains. Perception is immediate and direct, judging is made in steps. But that doesn't guarantee correctness of perception made by intuition.

For example, imagine same regular-sized rock but a person who encounters it is primarily oriented towards intuitive function. Instead of perceiving its sensory details, he might perceive it as something representing eternal time, or reach an immediate conclusion that there was a beach nearby before (without steps or deduction). To an outside observer, this might either feel too mystical or too absurd, depending on how accurate and abstract it was.


#### *iii. Thinking*

*Thinking* is one of the two judging functions. It judges or evaluates the information based on concepts and logical relations. It processes the contents of consciousness by evaluating what they are and how they connect with each other. Or in a more formal way, its a function which evaluates contents of consciousness whether subjective or objective, based on their structural magnitude. Something which is governed by rules and physical principles. Why physical principles or structural magnitude? Because whatever is considered logical, has some values attached to it, which will be cleared in next example. Why is it a judging function? Because the content in consciousness is not being simply registered as sensing or intuition do, but being evaluated based on concepts and logical relations. Unlike perception which is immediate, evaluation occurs in stages or steps, judging the content instead of just observing it.

For example, following the same story about a regular-sized rock and a person, this time a primarily thinking oriented person holds the rock, and evaluates it based on concepts and logical relations or rules. He focuses on the structure of rock, its diameter, particular color it has, and its magnitude of its weight as per measurement value. Unlike sensing where it was simply registered how the rock was perceived by the senses as, and intuition which saw a deeper meaning or potential in it beyond senses, thinking function evaluates it using concepts and logical relations.


#### *iv. Feeling*

*Feeling* is the other judging function. It judges or evaluates the information or contents in consciousness based on abstract importance or valuation. Meaning, it processes those information by evaluating what kind of abstract value if holds for the subject or in the object. A question might be about what kind of abstract value? And what part is abstract in it? Well, most people place certain value or importance in ideas, materials, or people, etc. Someone likes a certain kind of dress to wear, or places very much importance in certain ideology, etc. So it depends on whether the value is internal, or it adapts itself to objective conditions. Well, that's our future concern when we move on to study two types of feeling functions formed after attitudes are combined, point is that the values placed by a feeling function can't be logically deducted or inferred as it was in thinking function, being *"abstract"*. Why judging function? Because just like thinking, it is based on evaluation, and not immediate register of data based on perception. It evaluates the contents to decide if it must operate on it or not.

For example, following the same story about a regular-sized rock and a person, this time a primarily feeling oriented person holds the rock, and evaluates it based on its abstract importance or abstract value. It might put or evaluate it based on factors like *"is it good?"*, *"is it beautiful?"*, or even *"is it likeable?"* Now these might not make appear logical to many, but finding *beauty* or *likeableness* in something like rock, if assuming such happens, the processing involved is feeling oriented.



## V. Psychological Types


Its not true that any type exists in isolation. Rather they are backed by an auxiliary type and repress an inferior type, both will be discussed further in this chapter. These types can be called as type of individuals with certain natural orientation, or even types of processing styles. Just remember than *introversion* is about integration and subjective factor, *extroversion* is about adaptation and objective factor, *sensing* is about perception of sensory information, *intuition* is about perception of unconscious information, *thinking* is evaluating information in terms of logical concepts, and *feeling* is about evaluating information in terms of abstract importance or values.


#### *i. Introverted Sensing*

*Introverted sensing* is a type produced from simultaneous primary orientation towards an introverted attitude, and towards sensing function. Together they show up a certain kind of effect. We understood that sensing is about perceiving sensory information in contents of consciousness, while introversion is about flow of consciousness towards integration of information into larger subjective factor if they fit into it, instead of adapting to present external factors. So an introverted sensing type is in particular oriented towards the known sensory data. People with this orientation prefer not adapting to externally changing sensory factor, but stick to sensations which are known to them. That's why they often prefer to live in or work in environment that is familiar to them, and let's them be in touch of known sensory perceptions. And if they try to perceive any new object, they would process it by comparing it to their subjective factor (about sensations), and then integrate what they perceived to that factor. This type represses *extroverted intuition* (explained about it further), which comes up as a temporary orientation for psychological compensation.

For example, a house maid who likes her job of regularly visiting houses, carrying out chores there, returning and cooking meal for her husband in night, and then repeating this routine. Introverted sensation is not about routine, but its about orientation towards the subjective sensation factor. This example is particularly about the maid being comfortable with the repeated sensations she gets, because they are subjective and doesn't require her to adapt to externally changing sensory conditions, instead it lets her carry work by sticking to what she values the most, i.e. being in touch with known sensations.


#### *ii. Extroverted Sensing*

*Extroverted sensing* is a type produced from simultaneous primary orientation towards an extroverted attitude, and towards sensing function. We understood that sensing is about perceiving sensory information in contents of consciousness, while extroversion is about flow of consciousness towards adaptation to present conditions or the objective factor, instead of sticking to their subjective factor. So an extroverted sensing type is particularly oriented towards adaptation to constantly changing sensory conditions in their surrounding environment. Not only that, they would prefer to be in situation that requires them to constantly adapt to everchanging sensory factors. People with this orientation would more readily adapt to newer situations requiring sensory adaptability than *introverted sensing* types. And when they perceive any new object, they would process it by comparing it to their subjective factor (about sensations), but not integrate it by default to the subjective impression. This type represses *introverted intuition* (explained about it further), which comes up as a temporary orientation for psychological compensation.

For example, a rescuer who loves taking part in missions which are newer than previous ones, and require him to adapt with the new sensory factors he might encounter. Maybe its about newer terrain than his previous missions, or has newer physical challenges to go through, etc. Exposure to situations where same or similar sensory signatures are being followed might bore him, or cause disinterest, because this type naturally prefers to adapting with sensory aspects of an environment or system.


#### *iii. Introverted Intuition*

*Introverted intuition* is a type produced from simultaneous primary orientation towards an introverted attitude, and towards intuitive function. We understood that intuition is about perceiving unconscious information or images in contents of consciousness, while introversion is about flow of consciousness towards integration of information into larger subjective factor if they fit into it, instead of adapting to present external factors. So an introverted intuition type is particularly oriented towards the known or familiar unconscious information. They prefer to be in situation which requires them to spend time of unconscious details they are already aware of, instead of adapting to constantly changing objective factor from which they need to extract unconscious information. This is unlike *extroverted intuition* where they are required to adapt to changing situations in such a way. When it encounters a new or unfamiliar object, it perceives it by comparing it to known unconscious images stored as subjective factor, and integrates them to the singular impression if they fit within it. Also, this type represses the *extroverted sensing* type, which comes up as a temporary orientation for psychological compensation.

For example, a writer who takes pleasure in writing stories which reflect his subjective factor of unconscious images. There are certain abstract topics he broods on, which reflect what kind of unconscious understanding he possesses. He uses symbolic language in it, because those symbols are the images formed in his psyche when he recalls certain content. When he encounters a new object, he forms a larger singular underlying meaning, because introverted functions are integrative of new information. Its very same reason why many introverted intuitive types might appear existential. They keep extracting underlying meanings from objects, and keep merging or integrating them with a larger unconscious impression internally. And at some point, this leads to a universal worldview on matters for making a single understanding with varying sources. And they won't destabilize their subjective unconscious factors so easily.


#### *iv. Extroverted Intuition*

*Extroverted Intuition* is the psychological type produced from simultaneous primary orientation towards extroversion attitude and intuition function. Earlier we understood that intuition is about perceiving unconscious information or images in contents of consciousness, while extroversion is about flow of consciousness towards adaptation to present conditions or the objective factor, instead of sticking to their subjective factor. Together, it produces a combined orientation of type which prefers to adapt with respect to the unconscious images in objective factor or the external environment, instead of sticking with subjective factor of stored unconscious impressions. Unlike *introverted intuition* which sticks to subjective unconscious images and integrates certain unconscious images from objects to the singular internal impression, the extroverted intuition instead perceives the unconscious images in objective factor to adapt with them while not integrating them with the subjective impression by default. This type represses *introverted sensing* type, which comes up as a temporary orientation for psychological compensation.

For example, imagine a problem solver who prefers to be in challenging situations which require him adapting to their unconscious factors. Suddenly anything can change, or a new problem emerge in that chaotic situation. But this problem solver likes those exact situations where he understands what kind of potentials those isolated situations hold, and quickly responds to them. Not using force, not using logic, but by perceiving unconscious image about the object. And because it is not forming a singular impression by connecting all experiences altogether, it sees the independent potential an object holds, and how situations could develop into something (without reasoning or steps) or a sudden realization of a great idea.


#### *v. Introverted Thinking*
