# $\fbox{VEONS}$





## Abstract

**VEONS (Virtual Engine of Neurochemical States)** is a theoretical framework that models personality and cognition as emergent properties of interacting neurochemical systems. The framework proposes that psychological behavior can be understood through two fundamental attitudes: **Introversion** and **Extraversion**, and four cognitive functions: **Sensation**, **Intuition**, **Thinking**, and **Feeling**. Each attitude and function is represented as a structured configuration of neurochemicals whose relative levels influence the manner in which information is perceived, processed, evaluated, and acted upon.

Within VEONS, **Perception** consists of the functions of Sensation and Intuition, which acquire and represent information, while **Judgment** consists of Thinking and Feeling, which evaluate information and guide decision-making. Sensation is associated with immediate experience and bodily awareness, Intuition with abstraction and possibility generation, Thinking with logical analysis and consistency, and Feeling with value assignment and significance evaluation. Introversion and Extraversion are treated as orientations of psychological energy, directing attention primarily toward internal mental activity or external engagement respectively.

To formalize individual differences, VEONS introduces a notation system that represents cognitive functions as neurochemical profiles with varying levels of excess or deficiency. These profiles can be combined with attitudes to produce concise personality-type expressions, enabling systematic description of cognitive tendencies and behavioral patterns. The framework is intended as a conceptual and exploratory model that integrates neuroscience-inspired mechanisms with personality theory, providing a structured language for discussing cognition, temperament, and information processing across educational, research, and self-reflective contexts.



## 1. Terminologies


- **VEONS:** Virtual Engine Of Neurochemical States
- **Subject:** Conscious entity which is processing object
- **Object:** The topic of attention that subject is processing
- **Excess:** Too much of something
- **Deficiency:** Lack of something
- **Attitude:** Direction in which subject's energy flows (inward or outward)
- **Function:** Form of perception or judgement made on object.
- **Primary function:** Function that the subject primarily orients towards.
- **Secondary function:** Secondary function that aids primary function to process, but opposite in function type.



## 2. Attitudes


### 2.1 <u>Introversion (I)</u>:

**Introversion** is a cognitive orientation characterized by a preference for directing attention toward internal thoughts, memories, concepts, and mental models rather than toward continuous engagement with external stimuli. Neurochemically, it is associated with the combined influence of **acetylcholine** (deep focus and detailed internal processing), **serotonin** (contentment with existing conditions and reduced need for external stimulation), **melatonin** (periodic withdrawal for restoration and reflection), and **anandamide** (imagination, internal associations, and contemplative thinking).

As a result, introverted behavior tends to emphasize sustained concentration, reflection before action, comfort with solitude, rich inner experience, and the exploration of ideas within the mind. The individual often derives satisfaction from understanding, analyzing, or imagining rather than from constant interaction with the external environment. Introversion does not imply shyness or poor social skills; rather, it reflects a tendency for psychological energy to be invested primarily in internal processing and self-generated mental activity.

| ------------------------ | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| **Acetylcholine ($ac$)** | Neurochemical specialized for **deep, selective attention**, allowing sustained focus on internal thoughts, details, and mental models.              | Increased concentration, prolonged reflection, detailed analysis, ability to become deeply absorbed in a subject. | Distractibility, difficulty maintaining focus, shallow processing of ideas, reduced introspection.           |
| **Serotonin ($st$)**     | Neurochemical specialized for **contentment with the current state**, reducing the need to constantly seek new external stimulation.                 | Greater comfort with solitude, emotional stability, patience, satisfaction with existing circumstances.           | Restlessness, dissatisfaction, increased desire for external stimulation or change, emotional volatility.    |

| **Anandamide ($aa$)**    | Endocannabinoid specialized for **internal association and imaginative exploration**, helping connect ideas, memories, and concepts within the mind. | Rich imagination, reflective thinking, daydreaming, broad internal associations, creative contemplation.          | Narrower internal associations, reduced imagination, less reflective thought, decreased mental wandering.    |


### 2.2 <u>Extroversion (E)</u>:

**Extraversion** is a cognitive orientation characterized by a preference for directing attention toward the external environment, opportunities, people, and ongoing events rather than primarily toward internal reflection. Neurochemically, it is associated with the combined influence of **dopamine** (exploration, novelty-seeking, and opportunity pursuit), **norepinephrine** (external vigilance and environmental engagement), **testosterone** (assertiveness, initiative, and competitive action), and **orexin** (sustained energetic involvement with the world).

As a result, extraverted behavior tends to emphasize active engagement, exploration, responsiveness to external stimuli, social initiative, and action-oriented decision-making. The individual often derives motivation from interacting with people, pursuing opportunities, influencing events, and experiencing novelty. Extraversion does not imply superficiality or lack of introspection; rather, it reflects a tendency for psychological energy to be invested primarily in interacting with, adapting to, and affecting the external environment.

| ------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Dopamine ($da$)**       | Neurochemical specialized for **exploration, novelty-seeking, and engagement with opportunities**, motivating interaction with the external world. | Increased curiosity, initiative, exploration, enthusiasm for new experiences, active pursuit of goals.           | Reduced curiosity, passivity, lack of initiative, decreased interest in exploring opportunities.           |
| **Norepinephrine ($np$)** | Neurochemical specialized for **external vigilance and engagement**, directing attention toward important events and stimuli in the environment.   | Greater alertness, responsiveness, situational awareness, readiness to engage with surroundings.                 | Low alertness, reduced engagement, poor responsiveness to environmental changes, diminished attentiveness. |

| **Orexin ($or$)**         | Neurochemical specialized for **sustaining energetic interaction with the environment**, supporting active participation and wakeful engagement.   | High energy, sustained activity, persistence in engagement, strong drive to remain involved with ongoing events. | Fatigue, reduced engagement, excessive sleepiness, difficulty maintaining active participation.            |



## 3. Function Type


### 3.1 <u>Perception</u>:

**Perception** is the cognitive process responsible for acquiring, representing, and understanding information before it is judged or evaluated. Neurochemically, it is associated with the combined activity of the **sensation system** including histamine, endorphins, melatonin, orexin, and substance P which provides awareness of immediate experience, and the **intuition system** including dopamine, acetylcholine, BDNF, estrogen, and anandamide which transforms experience into patterns, concepts, and possibilities.

As a result, perception-oriented processing emphasizes observing, noticing, exploring, and understanding what exists or what could exist without immediately deciding whether it is good, bad, true, false, desirable, or undesirable. It gathers information from both the external world and the internal imagination, constructing representations of reality and potential reality. Rather than reaching conclusions or assigning value, perception primarily answers the question, **"What is there to know, observe, or imagine?"**.


### 3.2 <u>Judgement</u>:

**Judgment** is the cognitive process responsible for evaluating, organizing, prioritizing, and deciding what should be concluded, valued, or acted upon. Neurochemically, it is associated with the combined activity of the **thinking system** including glutamate, GABA, norepinephrine, D-serine, and aspartate which analyzes information for consistency and logical validity, and the **feeling system** including serotonin, oxytocin, vasopressin, prolactin, and cortisol which evaluates information according to significance, importance, relationships, and well-being.

As a result, judgment-oriented processing emphasizes reaching conclusions, establishing priorities, making decisions, and determining courses of action. It transforms information gathered through perception into beliefs, preferences, values, plans, and commitments. Rather than seeking additional information or possibilities, judgment primarily seeks resolution and direction, answering the question, **"Given what is known, what should be concluded, valued, or done?"**.



## 3. Functions & Neurochemicals Description


### 3.1 <u>Sensation (S)</u>:

**Sensation** is the cognitive function responsible for detecting, experiencing, and monitoring the immediate state of the body and environment. Neurochemically, it is associated with the combined influence of **histamine** (wakefulness and sensory alertness), **endorphins** (pain and comfort perception), **melatonin** (awareness of fatigue and circadian state), **orexin** (energy and wakeful engagement), and **substance P** (pain and bodily distress signaling).

As a result, sensation-oriented processing emphasizes awareness of what is happening in the present moment through direct experience. It provides information about comfort and discomfort, energy and fatigue, safety and danger, and changes occurring within the body or surroundings. Rather than interpreting or evaluating experiences, sensation primarily answers the question, **"What am I experiencing right now?"**, supplying the raw experiential data upon which higher cognitive processes operate.

| ---------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Histamine ($ha$)**   | Neurochemical responsible for **wakefulness and sensory alertness**, helping the brain remain responsive to environmental stimuli.      | Increased alertness, heightened awareness, greater responsiveness to sensory input, difficulty sleeping if excessive. | Drowsiness, reduced vigilance, mental sluggishness, decreased ability to maintain attention.                     |
| **Endorphins ($ed$)**  | Neurochemicals specialized for **pain reduction and physical comfort**, acting as the body's natural opioid system.                     | Reduced pain perception, increased resilience to discomfort, relaxation, feelings of well-being or mild euphoria.     | Increased sensitivity to pain, lower stress tolerance, reduced physical and emotional comfort.                   |
| **Melatonin ($mt$)**   | Hormone specialized for **signaling biological night and sleep readiness**, coordinating circadian rhythms.                             | Sleepiness, easier sleep initiation, stronger night-time recovery, excessive drowsiness if elevated.                  | Difficulty falling asleep, disrupted sleep schedule, insomnia-like symptoms, daytime fatigue from poor sleep.    |

### 3.2 <u>Intuition (N)</u>:

**Intuition** is the cognitive function responsible for discovering patterns, forming abstractions, generating possibilities, and connecting information beyond what is immediately observed. Neurochemically, it is associated with the combined influence of **dopamine** (novelty detection and exploration of possibilities), **acetylcholine** (attention to meaningful features and relationships), **BDNF** (formation and reorganization of conceptual structures), **estrogen** (cognitive flexibility and contextual integration), and **anandamide** (associative and divergent thinking).

As a result, intuition-oriented processing emphasizes recognizing underlying relationships, constructing mental models, imagining alternatives, and anticipating future developments. Instead of focusing on present sensory details, it seeks broader meanings, hidden patterns, and unexplored possibilities. Intuition often operates by synthesizing information from multiple sources into a coherent concept or insight, answering the question, **"What could this mean, and what else might be possible?"**.

| ------------------------ | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Dopamine ($da$)**      | Neurochemical specialized for **novelty detection, exploration, and hypothesis generation**, encouraging the search for new possibilities and patterns.    | Increased curiosity, creativity, exploration of ideas, pattern discovery, willingness to consider unconventional connections. | Reduced curiosity, rigid thinking, decreased exploration, difficulty generating new ideas or possibilities.             |
| **Acetylcholine ($ac$)** | Neurochemical specialized for **selective attention and feature extraction**, helping the brain identify relevant details from complex information.        | Enhanced focus on important details, improved learning, richer mental models, better recognition of subtle distinctions.      | Distractibility, difficulty learning new information, reduced mental clarity, poorer pattern recognition.               |
| **BDNF ($bd$)**          | Neurotrophic factor specialized for **forming and reorganizing neural connections**, enabling adaptation and the development of new conceptual structures. | Greater learning capacity, cognitive flexibility, easier acquisition of new concepts and skills.                              | Reduced adaptability, slower learning, difficulty restructuring beliefs or understanding new frameworks.                |
| **Estrogen ($eg$)**      | Hormone specialized for **context integration and cognitive flexibility**, helping the brain connect information across multiple domains and perspectives. | Improved contextual thinking, better integration of information, increased flexibility in interpretation.                     | More rigid thinking, reduced contextual awareness, difficulty shifting perspectives or integrating diverse information. |
| **Anandamide ($aa$)**    | Endocannabinoid specialized for **associative and divergent thinking**, allowing distant ideas and experiences to become connected.                        | Increased imaginative thinking, unusual associations, broader perspective, enhanced divergent thinking.                       | Narrower associations, reduced imagination, less flexibility in connecting seemingly unrelated concepts.                |


### 3.3 <u>Thinking (T)</u>:

**Thinking** is the cognitive function responsible for analyzing information, applying rules, evaluating consistency, and deriving conclusions from established facts or premises. Neurochemically, it is associated with the combined influence of **glutamate** (neural computation and information transmission), **GABA** (suppression of irrelevant activity and cognitive noise), **norepinephrine** (focused attention on relevant information), **D-serine** (precision in learning and model refinement), and **aspartate** (support of high-energy neural signaling and computation).

As a result, thinking-oriented processing emphasizes logical structure, objective analysis, causal reasoning, and systematic problem-solving. It seeks to determine what follows from the available information, identify contradictions, and organize knowledge into coherent frameworks. Rather than assigning personal significance or emotional value, thinking primarily focuses on accuracy, consistency, and validity, answering the question, **"Given what is known, what conclusion logically follows?"**.

| ------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Glutamate ($ga$)**      | Neurotransmitter specialized for **neural computation and information transmission**, forming the primary excitatory signaling system of the brain. | Faster information processing, improved learning, enhanced analytical capacity, greater mental activity.       | Mental fog, slower thinking, reduced learning efficiency, difficulty processing complex information.    |
| **GABA ($gb$)**           | Neurotransmitter specialized for **suppressing irrelevant neural activity**, improving the signal-to-noise ratio of thought.                        | Greater mental stability, reduced distraction, more orderly reasoning, improved concentration.                 | Racing thoughts, distractibility, cognitive noise, difficulty maintaining coherent reasoning.           |

| **D-Serine ($ds$)**       | Neuromodulator specialized for **supporting precise learning and synaptic refinement**, helping the brain update and validate internal models.      | Improved reasoning accuracy, enhanced learning, better integration of new information with existing knowledge. | Reduced cognitive precision, slower learning, difficulty refining or correcting mental models.          |
| **Aspartate ($as$)**      | Excitatory neurotransmitter specialized for **supporting high-energy neural signaling and computational activity** alongside glutamate.             | Increased neural responsiveness, enhanced cognitive activation, improved processing speed.                     | Reduced cognitive activation, slower information processing, diminished mental responsiveness.          |


### 3.4 <u>Feeling (F)</u>:

**Feeling** is the cognitive function responsible for assigning value, significance, priority, and meaning to people, experiences, ideas, and outcomes. Neurochemically, it is associated with the combined influence of **serotonin** (satisfaction and well-being), **oxytocin** (trust and social connection), **vasopressin** (loyalty, commitment, and protection), **prolactin** (caregiving and nurturing value), and **cortisol** (evaluation of threats, risks, and importance).

As a result, feeling-oriented processing emphasizes determining what matters, what should be cared about, what should be protected, and how different situations affect well-being and relationships. It evaluates experiences not primarily by their logical validity but by their personal, social, ethical, or emotional significance. Rather than asking what is true or what is possible, feeling primarily answers the question, **"What is valuable, meaningful, or important?"**.

## 4. Type Notation Rules


### 4.1 <u>Chemical Levels</u>:

- Excess: $+$
- Deficient: $-$

>*Even better if we use scales instead like $[-2,2]$, or $[0,10]$, etc.*


### 4.2 <u>Function Dissection (Schema)</u>:

$$ function(chem1^{+/-},chem2^{+/-},chem3^{+/-},chem4^{+/-},chem5^{+/-}) $$


### 4.3 <u>Function Dissection (Example)</u>:

Let's say the chosen function is **Intuition ($N$)**:
$$ N(da^{+},ac^{+},bd^{-},eg^{+},aa^{-}) $$

- High ($+$) dopamine ($da$)
- High ($+$) acetylcholine ($ac$)
- Low ($-$) BDNF ($bd$)
- High ($+$) estrogen ($eg$)
- Low ($-$) anandamide ($aa$)


### 4.4 <u>Type Notation (Schema)</u>:

$$ \text{primaryFunction + secondaryFunction + mainAttitude} $$


### 4.5 <u>Type Notation (Example)</u>:

Let's say a type has primary function as thinking ($T$), secondary as sensing ($S$), and an attitude of introversion ($I$):

$$ \text{T + S + I = TSI} $$


### 4.6 <u>Complete Typing Procedure</u>:

$$ \sigma = \sqrt{\frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{n}{(x_i - \mu)^2}} $$

1. The person typing the person is provided with the list of neurochemicals for each primitive ($I$/$E$/$S$/$N$/$T$/$F$).
2. For chemicals listed for each primitive, the typologist rates them on a scale of $[-2,2]$ where $-2$ means extreme deficiency & $2$ means too excess.
3. Sum is made for the chemicals existing in each of the primitive separately.
4. For the functions, the primitive with highest score is chosen to be the first letter.
5. If it draws in score with another function of different type, then we choose of the two which shares the same function type as the lowest scoring one.
6. Else if two highest scoring functions share the same score, then whichever has lower absolute standard deviation ranks first.
7. Then the one ranking second after applying rules above must be the second letter.
8. For third letter, we will compare scores of both the attitudes, and the one scoring more would be it.
9. If they score equal, then again a absolute standard deviation test is made and the one having lower deviation is chosen.



## 5. Important Notes


### 5.1 <u>For Mental Health Professionals</u>:

VEONS is a **conceptual cognitive framework** intended to describe patterns of information processing, valuation, and behavioral orientation through neurochemically inspired constructs. It is **not a diagnostic instrument**, psychometric assessment, clinical taxonomy, or validated model of personality, psychopathology, or neurobiology. The neurochemical associations presented within the framework are functional abstractions designed to aid conceptual understanding and should not be interpreted as direct measurements of neurotransmitter or hormone levels in an individual.

The framework does not claim that specific personality traits, cognitive functions, or attitudes are caused solely by the neurochemicals assigned to them. Human cognition and behavior arise from highly complex interactions among neural networks, genetics, development, environment, culture, learning, and physiology. Consequently, VEONS should not be used to infer biological abnormalities, diagnose mental disorders, prescribe treatments, evaluate clinical risk, or make decisions regarding patient care.

Mental health professionals who choose to explore the framework are encouraged to treat it as a theoretical lens for discussing cognitive tendencies and subjective experiences rather than as a representation of established neuroscience. Any clinical interpretation, assessment, or intervention should rely upon scientifically validated diagnostic criteria, evidence-based practices, and professional judgment rather than the classifications or notation systems described in VEONS.


### 5.2 <u>For Educators</u>:

VEONS may be used as an educational framework for discussing differences in learning styles, information processing preferences, problem-solving approaches, and motivational tendencies. By distinguishing between perception and judgment, as well as between sensation, intuition, thinking, and feeling, the framework provides a structured vocabulary for understanding how students may approach learning, decision-making, and engagement with educational material.

The framework should not be used to label, rank, limit, or stereotype learners. A student's VEONS profile, if applied, should be viewed as a description of preferred cognitive tendencies rather than a measure of intelligence, academic potential, creativity, emotional maturity, or future success. Individuals are capable of developing skills outside their preferred orientations, and educational opportunities should encourage balanced growth across multiple cognitive domains.

Educators are encouraged to use VEONS as a tool for promoting self-awareness, communication, and instructional diversity. The framework may assist in designing varied learning experiences that accommodate different approaches to observation, abstraction, analysis, and valuation. However, educational decisions, assessments, and interventions should remain grounded in established pedagogical research, empirical evidence, and professional educational practice rather than relying solely on VEONS classifications.


### 5.3 <u>For Researchers</u>:

VEONS is presented as a speculative and interdisciplinary framework intended to stimulate discussion at the intersection of personality theory, cognitive science, neuroscience, psychology, systems modeling, and human behavior. The framework proposes a structured mapping between cognitive functions and neurochemically inspired mechanisms, not as established biological fact, but as a conceptual model for exploring how information acquisition, abstraction, reasoning, valuation, and behavioral orientation may interact within a unified system.

Researchers should not interpret the neurochemical assignments within VEONS as experimentally verified one-to-one relationships. The functions, attitudes, and notation system are abstractions designed to simplify highly complex biological and psychological processes. Many of the neurochemicals included in the framework participate in multiple neural systems and behavioral functions simultaneously, and the categorizations used by VEONS prioritize conceptual clarity over biological completeness.

The framework is intended to generate hypotheses rather than confirm them. Researchers are encouraged to critically evaluate, challenge, modify, expand, or empirically test the assumptions presented within VEONS. Any scientific investigation involving cognition, personality, behavior, or neurochemistry should rely on rigorous methodology, reproducible evidence, and established scientific standards. VEONS should therefore be viewed as a theoretical construct that may inspire future inquiry rather than as a validated scientific model.


### 5.4 <u>For Hobbyists & General Users</u>:

VEONS is intended as a framework for self-exploration, discussion, and understanding differences in cognition, personality, and behavior. It provides a structured language for thinking about how individuals perceive information, form abstractions, reason through problems, assign value, and orient their attention toward either internal or external experience. The framework is designed to encourage curiosity about the diversity of human thought rather than to define or limit personal identity.

Users should avoid treating VEONS classifications as fixed labels or permanent descriptions of themselves or others. Cognitive tendencies can vary across contexts, stages of life, learning experiences, emotional states, and environmental conditions. A VEONS type should therefore be understood as a simplified model of preference and orientation rather than a complete representation of a person's character, abilities, beliefs, or potential.

The framework is most useful when applied as a tool for reflection and communication. It can help individuals better understand their strengths, blind spots, preferred learning approaches, decision-making styles, and interactions with others. However, VEONS should not be used to justify behavior, predict outcomes with certainty, evaluate personal worth, or replace professional advice in educational, medical, psychological, or occupational matters. Its primary purpose is to promote insight, discussion, and exploration of human cognition through an accessible conceptual model.
